The concept of retrieval augmented generation has gained significant attention in recent years, particularly in the context of reducing hallucination in large language model outputs (Shuster and Poff, 2021). Hallucination refers to the phenomenon where language models generate text that is not grounded in reality (Zhang and Li, 2023). Retrieval augmented generation involves using external knowledge sources to inform the generation process, thereby reducing the likelihood of hallucination (Kamra and Gupta, 2025). 

Several studies have explored the application of retrieval augmented generation in various domains, including conversation (Shuster and Poff, 2021) and image-to-text matching (Bernardi and Cimitile, 2024). For instance, Shuster and Poff (2021) demonstrated that retrieval augmentation can reduce hallucination in conversation by providing the model with relevant context. Similarly, Bernardi and Cimitile (2024) showed that retrieval augmented generation can improve the accuracy of report generation from X-ray imaging.

Other studies have investigated the capabilities of large language models, including GPT-4, in various tasks (OpenAI, Wijayagunawardhana, et al., 2023; Nori and King, 2023). These studies have highlighted the potential of large language models, but also noted their limitations, including the tendency to hallucinate (Zhang and Li, 2023). In contrast, some studies have explored the use of retrieval augmented generation for personalizing large language models (Prahlad and Lee, 2025) and improving their performance in specific domains (Kamra and Gupta, 2025).

There is a general agreement among researchers that retrieval augmented generation has the potential to reduce hallucination in large language model outputs (Shuster and Poff, 2021; Zhang and Li, 2023). However, there are also some gaps in the existing literature, including the need for more studies on the application of retrieval augmented generation in different domains (Raiaan and Mukta, 2024). Furthermore, some studies have noted that the use of retrieval augmented generation can also introduce new challenges, such as the need for high-quality external knowledge sources (Prahlad and Lee, 2025).
