The integration of large language models in automated academic peer review and citation generation has sparked intense debate regarding its ethical implications. According to Dwivedi and Kshetri (2023), the use of generative conversational AI, such as ChatGPT, poses significant challenges and opportunities for research, practice, and policy. Lund and Wang (2023) concur, highlighting the ethics of large language models in scholarly publishing, particularly with regards to AI-written research papers. In contrast, Greenhalgh and Wherton (2017) focus on the challenges of adopting and scaling up health and care technologies, which may have implications for the development of large language models in academic settings.

The potential consequences of relying on large language models in education and research have also been explored. Rahman and Watanobe (2023) discuss the opportunities and threats of using ChatGPT in educational settings, while Grassini (2023) examines the potential and consequences of AI and ChatGPT in education. Similarly, Zhai and Chu (2021) review the role of artificial intelligence in education from 2010 to 2020, highlighting the need for careful consideration of the ethical implications. Perkins (2023) specifically addresses academic integrity considerations of AI large language models in the post-pandemic era, emphasizing the need for vigilance in maintaining academic standards.

There are, however, gaps in the existing literature. For instance, the potential impact of large language models on citation generation and academic peer review has not been fully explored. Furthermore, the long-term consequences of relying on AI-generated content in academic settings remain unclear. Despite these gaps, the existing literature highlights the need for careful consideration of the ethical implications of large language models in academic settings.
