The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) in various fields has sparked intense debate, with some authors highlighting its potential benefits (Dwivedi and Kshetri, 2023; Bibri and Krogstie, 2023) while others raise concerns about its implications (Resnik and Hosseini, 2024; Williamson and Prybutok, 2024). In the context of autonomous self-correcting AI agents for peer review validation and academic citation fraud detection, several papers touch on the theme of AI in research and its potential applications. For instance, Dwivedi and Kshetri (2023) discuss the opportunities and challenges of generative conversational AI, which could be leveraged for peer review validation. Similarly, Resnik and Hosseini (2024) emphasize the need for new guidance on the ethics of using AI in scientific research, which is crucial for ensuring the integrity of peer review processes.

The use of AI in detecting academic citation fraud is also a relevant theme, with papers such as Qin and Xu (2023) and Asudani and Nagwani (2023) discussing the applications of AI in text analytics and economic development. However, there is a gap in the literature regarding the specific application of AI in detecting citation fraud. Furthermore, the papers by Saarela and Podgorelec (2024) and De Bock and Coussement (2023) highlight the importance of explainable AI (XAI) in various fields, which could be applied to peer review validation and citation fraud detection.

In contrast, some papers focus on the challenges and limitations of AI in various fields, such as healthcare (Williamson and Prybutok, 2024) and supply management (Spreitzenbarth and Bode, 2024). These papers highlight the need for careful consideration of the implications of AI and the importance of addressing potential challenges and limitations. Overall, the literature suggests that AI has the potential to be a valuable tool in peer review validation and citation fraud detection, but further research is needed to fully explore its applications and limitations.
