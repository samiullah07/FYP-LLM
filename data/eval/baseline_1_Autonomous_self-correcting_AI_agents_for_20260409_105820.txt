The development of autonomous self-correcting AI agents for peer review validation and academic citation fraud detection has gained significant attention in recent years (Dwivedi et al., 2023; Bibri and Krogstie, 2023). The integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) in academic publishing has the potential to improve the efficiency and accuracy of the peer review process (Resnik and Hosseini, 2024). However, it also raises concerns about the ethics of using AI in scientific research (Resnik and Hosseini, 2024) and the potential for AI-generated content to perpetuate academic citation fraud (Dwivedi et al., 2023).

Furthermore, the development of Explainable AI (XAI) has gained traction, with applications in operational research (De Bock and Coussement, 2023) and systematic literature reviews (Saarela and Podgorelec, 2024).

In the context of peer review validation and academic citation fraud detection, autonomous self-correcting AI agents can play a crucial role in identifying and correcting errors, as well as detecting potential fraud (Witt and Heyer, 2022). The use of decentralized and incentivized federated learning frameworks can also improve the accuracy and efficiency of AI-powered peer review systems (Witt and Heyer, 2022). Additionally, the integration of blockchain technology and wearable devices can enhance the security and trust management of medical data (Murala and Panda, 2023), which can also be applied to peer review systems.

The use of AI and ML in purchasing and supply management has also been explored, with a focus on the state-of-the-art in literature and practice (Spreitzenbarth and Bode, 2024). In conclusion, the development of autonomous self-correcting AI agents for peer review validation and academic citation fraud detection is a rapidly evolving field, with significant potential for improving the efficiency and accuracy of the peer review process. However, it also raises concerns about the ethics of using AI in scientific research and the potential for AI-generated content to perpetuate academic citation fraud. Further research is needed to explore the applications and limitations of AI and ML in peer review systems, as well as the development of new guidance and regulations for the use of AI in scientific research.
