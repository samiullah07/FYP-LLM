The integration of large language models in automated academic peer review and citation generation has sparked concerns regarding ethical implications. According to Dwivedi and Kshetri (2023), the use of generative conversational AI, such as ChatGPT, raises questions about authorship and the potential for biased or inaccurate content. Similarly, Lund and Wang (2023) highlight the ethics of large language models in scholarly publishing, emphasizing the need for transparency and accountability. In contrast, Rahman and Watanobe (2023) focus on the opportunities presented by ChatGPT in educational settings, while also acknowledging the potential threats to academic integrity.

The issue of academic integrity is further explored by Perkins (2023), who discusses the considerations of AI large language models in the post-pandemic era. However, there is a gap in the literature regarding the specific implications of large language models on automated academic peer review and citation generation. Greenhalgh and Wherton (2017) provide a framework for evaluating the challenges and sustainability of health and care technologies, which could be applied to the context of large language models in academic settings. Nevertheless, there is a lack of research on the potential consequences of relying on AI-generated content in academic publishing.

In terms of agreements, Dwivedi and Ismagilova (2020) emphasize the importance of considering the social and ethical implications of digital and social media marketing research, which is also relevant to the context of large language models in academic settings. Conversely, there are contradictions between the optimistic views of ChatGPT's potential in education (Rahman and Watanobe, 2023; Grassini, 2023) and the more cautious approaches highlighted by Lund and Wang (2023) and Perkins (2023). Further research is needed to address these gaps and contradictions, and to fully explore the ethical implications of large language models in automated academic peer review and citation generation.
