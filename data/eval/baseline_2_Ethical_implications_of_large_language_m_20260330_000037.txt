The integration of large language models in automated academic peer review and citation generation has sparked intense debate regarding its ethical implications (Dwivedi and Kshetri, 2023; Lund and Wang, 2023). A key concern is the potential for bias in the review process, as highlighted by Goldin and Rouse (2000) in their study on the impact of "blind" auditions on female musicians. This concern is also echoed by Fiske and Henningsen (2019) in their discussion on the ethical implications of embodied artificial intelligence in psychiatry, psychology, and psychotherapy. In contrast, some researchers argue that large language models can enhance the efficiency and accuracy of peer review (Dwivedi and Ismagilova, 2020; Rahman and Watanobe, 2023).

The use of artificial intelligence in academic settings also raises questions about academic integrity, as noted by Perkins (2023) and Lund and Wang (2023). Furthermore, the adoption of large language models in education and research may have unintended consequences, such as the potential for students to rely on AI-generated content rather than developing their own critical thinking skills (Grassini, 2023; Jenkins, 2006). Greenhalgh and Wherton (2017) provide a framework for evaluating the challenges and implications of adopting new technologies, which could be applied to the integration of large language models in academic settings.

There is a need for further research on the ethical implications of large language models in automated academic peer review and citation generation, as highlighted by the contradictions and gaps in the existing literature. For example, while some studies focus on the benefits of large language models (Dwivedi and Ismagilova, 2020; Rahman and Watanobe, 2023), others raise concerns about their potential impact on academic integrity (Perkins, 2023; Lund and Wang, 2023). A more comprehensive understanding of these implications is necessary to ensure that the adoption of large language models in academic settings is both effective and ethical.
