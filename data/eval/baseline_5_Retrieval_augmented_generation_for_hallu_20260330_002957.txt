The concept of retrieval augmented generation has gained significant attention in recent years as a means to reduce hallucination in large language model outputs (Shuster and Poff, 2021). Hallucination refers to the phenomenon where language models generate text that is not grounded in reality (Zhang and Li, 2023). Retrieval augmented generation involves using external knowledge sources to inform and constrain the generation process, thereby reducing the likelihood of hallucination (Kamra and Gupta, 2025). 

Studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of retrieval augmented generation in various applications, including conversation (Shuster and Poff, 2021) and report generation from X-ray imaging (Bernardi and Cimitile, 2024). The use of retrieval augmented generation has also been explored in the context of personalizing large language models, where it has been shown to improve performance and reduce hallucination (Prahlad and Lee, 2025). 

In contrast, some studies have highlighted the challenges and limitations of retrieval augmented generation, including the need for high-quality external knowledge sources and the potential for bias in the retrieval process (Naveed and Khan, 2023). Additionally, the effectiveness of retrieval augmented generation may depend on the specific application and task, with some studies suggesting that it may not always be effective in reducing hallucination (Choi and Chang, 2025). 

Despite these challenges, the use of retrieval augmented generation remains a promising approach for reducing hallucination in large language model outputs. Further research is needed to fully explore the potential of this approach and to address the challenges and limitations that have been identified.
