The concept of retrieval augmented generation has gained significant attention in recent years, particularly in the context of reducing hallucinations in large language models (Wan Zhang and Jing Zhang, 2025). Hallucinations refer to the phenomenon where language models generate text that is not grounded in reality, which can have severe consequences in applications such as medical education and clinical decision support (Josip Vrdoljak, Zvonimir Boban, Marino Vilović et al., 2025). 

Research has shown that retrieval augmentation can be an effective approach to mitigating hallucinations in language models. For instance, Kurt Shuster, Spencer Poff, Moya Chen et al. (2021) demonstrated that retrieval augmentation can reduce hallucination in conversation. Similarly, Gongbo Zhang, Zihan Xu, Qiao Jin et al. (2025) leveraged long context in retrieval augmented language models for medical question answering, highlighting the potential of this approach in real-world applications.

However, there is a need for further research in this area, particularly in understanding the mechanics behind large language models (Andrea Filippo Ferraris, Davide Audrito, Luigi Di et al., 2025) and their ability to encode clinical knowledge (Karan Singhal, Shekoofeh Azizi, Tao Tu et al., 2023). Additionally, the role of artificial intelligence in clinical practice (Shuroug A. Alowais, Sahar S. Alghamdi, Nada Alsuhebany et al., 2023) and education (Enkelejda Kasneci, Kathrin Seßler, Stefan Küchemann et al., 2023) requires further exploration.

In terms of methodologies, researchers have explored various approaches, including prompting methods (Pengfei Liu, Weizhe Yuan, Jinlan Fu et al., 2022) and iterative self-feedback (Yanming Liu, Xinyue Peng, Xuhong Zhang et al., 2024). However, there is a need for more systematic surveys of these methods and their applications in retrieval augmented generation.
