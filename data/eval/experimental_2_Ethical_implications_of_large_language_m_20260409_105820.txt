Literature Review: Ethical Implications of Large Language Models in Automated Academic Peer Review and Citation Generation

The integration of large language models (LLMs) in automated academic peer review and citation generation has sparked intense debate regarding their ethical implications. This review aims to synthesize the existing literature on the topic, highlighting agreements, contradictions, and research gaps.

**Opportunities and Challenges**

LLMs have been shown to possess the ability to encode clinical knowledge (Singhal et al., 2023). However, their use in automated peer review and citation generation raises concerns regarding the potential for bias and inaccuracy (Dwivedi et al., 2019). A systematic review of research on artificial intelligence applications in higher education found that educators are often left out of the development process, leading to a lack of understanding and trust in AI systems (Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019).

**Academic Integrity and Reproducibility**

The use of LLMs in citation generation has also raised concerns regarding academic integrity. A manifesto for reproducible science emphasizes the importance of transparency and accountability in scientific research (Munafò et al., 2017). However, the use of LLMs in citation generation may compromise these values, as it is difficult to determine the accuracy and reliability of the generated citations (Cotton et al., 2023).

**Social and Ethical Implications**

The use of LLMs in automated peer review and citation generation also raises social and ethical implications. A survey on sentiment analysis methods, applications, and challenges highlights the potential for LLMs to perpetuate biases and stereotypes (Wankhade et al., 2022). Furthermore, the use of LLMs in citation generation may exacerbate existing power dynamics in academia, as those with access to LLMs may have an unfair advantage over those without (Dwivedi et al., 2023).

**Research Gaps and Future Directions**

Despite the growing body of research on LLMs, there are still significant research gaps and challenges to be addressed. A systematic review of research on AI applications in higher education highlights the need for more research on the impact of AI on educator roles and responsibilities (Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019). Furthermore, the development of more transparent and accountable LLMs is essential to ensuring the integrity and reliability of automated peer review and citation generation (Munafò et al., 2017).

In conclusion, the use of LLMs in automated academic peer review and citation generation raises significant ethical implications. While LLMs have the potential to improve the efficiency and accuracy of peer review and citation generation, their use also raises concerns regarding bias, inaccuracy, and academic integrity. Further research is needed to address these challenges and ensure the responsible development and use of LLMs in academic settings.
