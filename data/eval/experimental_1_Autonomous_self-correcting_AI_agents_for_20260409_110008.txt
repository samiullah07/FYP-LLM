Literature Review: Autonomous Self-Correcting AI Agents for Peer Review Validation and Academic Citation Fraud Detection

The integration of artificial intelligence (AI) in higher education has been a topic of interest in recent years (Olaf Zawacki-Richter et al., 2019). AI applications have the potential to enhance the peer review process, improve the accuracy of citation validation, and detect academic citation fraud. However, the development of autonomous self-correcting AI agents requires a comprehensive understanding of the underlying technologies and their limitations.

Machine learning and deep learning have emerged as key technologies in AI research (Christian Janiesch et al., 2021). These techniques have been successfully applied in various domains, including natural language processing, computer vision, and speech recognition (Ali Bou Nassif et al., 2019). However, the development of autonomous self-correcting AI agents requires the integration of multiple AI techniques, including natural language processing, computer vision, and machine learning.

The use of AI in peer review validation and citation fraud detection has been explored in various studies (Yogesh K. Dwivedi et al., 2023). These studies have highlighted the potential benefits of AI in improving the accuracy and efficiency of peer review processes. However, the development of autonomous self-correcting AI agents requires the integration of multiple AI techniques and the development of robust evaluation metrics.

The development of autonomous self-correcting AI agents also requires the integration of multiple data sources and the development of robust data preprocessing techniques (Pauli Virtanen et al., 2020). The use of open-source software development frameworks, such as Bioconductor (Robert Gentleman et al., 2004), can facilitate the development of autonomous self-correcting AI agents.

The development of autonomous self-correcting AI agents also requires the consideration of ethical and social implications (Yogesh K. Dwivedi et al., 2019). The use of AI in peer review validation and citation fraud detection raises concerns about the potential biases and errors in AI decision-making processes.

In conclusion, the development of autonomous self-correcting AI agents for peer review validation and academic citation fraud detection requires a comprehensive understanding of the underlying technologies and their limitations. The integration of multiple AI techniques, the development of robust evaluation metrics, and the consideration of ethical and social implications are essential for the development of effective autonomous self-correcting AI agents.
