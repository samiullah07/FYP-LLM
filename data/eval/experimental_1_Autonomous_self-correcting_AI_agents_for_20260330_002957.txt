The development of autonomous self-correcting AI agents for peer review validation and academic citation fraud detection is a rapidly evolving field. According to Yogesh K. Dwivedi et al. (2023), generative conversational AI has the potential to transform research, practice, and policy, but it also poses significant challenges. One of the key challenges is ensuring academic integrity in the era of ChatGPT, as highlighted by Debby Cotton et al. (2023). 

The use of AI in academia has been explored in various studies, including a systematic review of research on artificial intelligence applications in higher education by Olaf Zawacki‐Richter et al. (2019). However, the specific application of AI in peer review validation and citation fraud detection is a relatively new area of research. A preliminary review of ChatGPT and Open-AI models by Konstantinos I. Roumeliotis et al. (2023) highlights the potential of these models for various applications, including academic writing.

The emergence of large language models has significant implications for academia, as noted by Jesse G. Meyer et al. (2023). These models can be used to generate high-quality text, but they also pose a risk of academic fraud. Cecilia Ka Yuk Chan et al. (2023) have explored students' perceptions of generative AI in higher education, highlighting both the benefits and challenges of these technologies.

In terms of the technical capabilities of AI models, a review of large language models by Mohaimenul Azam Khan Raiaan et al. (2024) provides an overview of the architectures, applications, and challenges of these models. However, the specific application of these models in peer review validation and citation fraud detection requires further research.
