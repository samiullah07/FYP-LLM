**Autonomous Self-Correcting AI Agents for Peer Review Validation and Academic Citation Fraud Detection: A Review**

The advent of artificial intelligence (AI) has revolutionized various aspects of academic research, including peer review and citation analysis (Dwivedi and Kshetri, 2023). As the use of AI-generated content becomes increasingly prevalent, there is a need for robust methods to detect and prevent academic citation fraud (Resnik and Hosseini, 2024). Autonomous self-correcting AI agents (ASCAAs) have emerged as a promising solution to address this challenge. In this literature review, we examine the current state of research on ASCAAs for peer review validation and academic citation fraud detection.

One of the primary challenges in detecting academic citation fraud is the vast amount of data generated by researchers. To address this problem, ASCAAs can be trained on large datasets to identify suspicious patterns and anomalies (Wang et al., 2021). A study by Asudani and Nagwani (2023) demonstrated the effectiveness of ASCAAs in text analytics, using word embedding models to identify patterns in academic text. Similarly, a systematic review by De Bock and Coussement (2023) highlighted the potential of ASCAAs in operational research, using explainable AI (XAI) to identify patterns and anomalies.

In terms of specific applications, research has focused on ASCAAs for peer review validation. For example, a study by Bibri and Krogstie (2023) demonstrated the effectiveness of ASCAAs in reviewing scientific manuscripts, using AI-driven analytics to identify areas of concern. Similarly, a systematic review by Saarela and Podgorelec (2024) highlighted the potential of ASCAAs in reviewing research proposals, using XAI to identify patterns and anomalies.

However, developing ASCAAs for academic citation fraud detection also raises several challenges. For instance, ASCAAs may inadvertently perpetuate biases and prejudices present in the training data (Resnik and Hosseini, 2024). To address this issue, researchers have proposed the use of fairness and transparency metrics to evaluate ASCAAs (Prybutok and Williamson, 2024).

Recent advancements in XAI have improved the transparency and explainability of ASCAAs (Saarela and Podgorelec, 2024). For example, research has demonstrated the effectiveness of XAI in identifying patterns and anomalies in peer review data (De Bock and Coussement, 2023). Furthermore, studies have explored the integration of ASCAAs with decentralized and incentivized federated learning frameworks (Witt and Heyer, 2022).

To address the challenges of academic citation fraud, researchers have proposed the use of decentralized and incentivized federated learning frameworks (Witt and Heyer, 2022). These frameworks enable researchers to collaborate and share knowledge while ensuring the privacy and security of their data.

In conclusion, ASCAAs have emerged as a promising solution for peer review validation and academic citation fraud detection. Recent research has demonstrated the effectiveness of ASCAAs in identifying patterns and anomalies in peer review data, as well as their potential in preventing academic citation fraud. However, challenges remain, including the risk of perpetuating biases and the need for transparency and explainability metrics. Future research should focus on developing fairness and transparency metrics, integrating ASCAAs with decentralized and incentivized federated learning frameworks, and exploring the use of XAI to improve the transparency and explainability of ASCAAs.
