The concept of multi-agent orchestration frameworks for knowledge-intensive reasoning and automated literature synthesis has garnered significant attention in recent years. In the realm of artificial intelligence, researchers have explored its applications in various domains, including supply chain management (Reza Toorajipour and Vahid Sohrabpour, 2020) and medical imaging informatics (Andreas S. Panayides and Amir A. Amini, 2020). The use of large language models has also been investigated, with studies focusing on their potential in autonomous agents (Lei Wang and Chen Ma, 2024) and retrieval-augmented generation (Yunfan Gao and Yun Xiong, 2023).

The integration of artificial intelligence with other technologies, such as edge computing, has been identified as a key area of research (Zhi Zhou and Xu Chen, 2019). This is particularly relevant in the context of service-oriented middleware, where researchers have explored the potential of artificial intelligence in enabling more efficient and effective services (Valérie Issarny and Nikolaos Georgantas, 2011). However, there are also concerns about the impact of technological disruptions on various industries, including tourism and hospitality (Dimitrios Buhalis and Tracy Harwood, 2019).

While there is agreement on the potential of artificial intelligence to drive innovation and improve business value (Ida Merete Enholm and Emmanouil Papagiannidis, 2021; Salman Bahoo and Marco Cucculelli, 2022), there are also contradictions in the literature regarding the capabilities required for effective human-AI collaboration (Lina Markauskaitė and Rebecca Marrone, 2022). Furthermore, there are gaps in the literature regarding the application of multi-agent orchestration frameworks in specific domains, such as education (Petros Lameras and Sylvester Arnab, 2021) and materials science (Rohit Batra and Le Song, 2020).
