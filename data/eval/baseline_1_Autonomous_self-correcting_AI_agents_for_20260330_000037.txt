The integration of autonomous self-correcting AI agents in academic research has sparked intense debate, with some authors highlighting the opportunities and challenges associated with this technology (Dwivedi and Kshetri, 2023). In the context of peer review validation, AI agents can facilitate the detection of academic citation fraud by analyzing patterns and anomalies in citation data (Resnik and Hosseini, 2024). However, the use of AI in scientific research also raises ethical concerns, such as the potential for bias and the need for transparency (Resnik and Hosseini, 2024).

The application of AI in various fields, including healthcare (Wang and Su, 2021; Williamson and Prybutok, 2024) and environmental sustainability (Bibri and Krogstie, 2023), has been extensively reviewed. These studies highlight the potential of AI to drive progress and improve outcomes, but also emphasize the need for careful consideration of privacy and oversight challenges (Williamson and Prybutok, 2024). The use of explainable AI (XAI) has been proposed as a solution to address these concerns, with recent reviews highlighting its applications and benefits (Saarela and Podgorelec, 2024; De Bock and Coussement, 2023).

While there is agreement on the potential of AI to transform various fields, there are also contradictions and gaps in the existing literature. For example, some authors emphasize the need for guidance on the use of AI in scientific research (Resnik and Hosseini, 2024), while others highlight the importance of integrating digital technologies and public health to address global challenges (Wang and Su, 2021). Further research is needed to address these gaps and ensure that the development and deployment of autonomous self-correcting AI agents are aligned with ethical and societal values.
