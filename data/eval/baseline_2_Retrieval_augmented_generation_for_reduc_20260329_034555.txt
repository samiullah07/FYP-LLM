The integration of retrieval-augmented generation in large language models has emerged as a promising approach to mitigate hallucinations, which are defined as instances where a model generates information not grounded in reality (Tran and Vijai, 2024). Hallucinations can compromise the reliability and trustworthiness of large language models, particularly in high-stakes applications such as medical education, clinical decision support, and healthcare administration (Vrdoljak and Boban, 2025). Recent studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of retrieval-augmented generation in reducing hallucinations and improving the overall performance of large language models (Joseph, 2025; Zhang and Zhang, 2025).

The concept of retrieval-augmented generation involves combining the strengths of generative models with those of retrieval-based approaches, leveraging external knowledge sources to inform and validate generated text (Lála and O'Donoghue, 2023). This integrated approach has been shown to enhance the accuracy and reliability of large language models in various domains, including scientific research (Lála and O'Donoghue, 2023), medical question answering (Zhang and Xu, 2025), and legal research (Magesh and Surani, 2025).

Furthermore, research has highlighted the importance of customized chatbots and retrieval-augmented generation in specific domains, such as cardiology (Tran and Vijai, 2024) and higher education (Neumann and Yin, 2024). The development of hallucination-aware tuning pipelines, such as RAG-HAT, has also been proposed to further mitigate hallucinations in retrieval-augmented generation (Song and Wang, 2024). Overall, the literature suggests that retrieval-augmented generation holds significant promise for reducing hallucinations in large language models and improving their performance in a range of applications.
